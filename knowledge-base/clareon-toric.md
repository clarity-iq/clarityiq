# Clareon Toric — Patient Labeling

**1. Metadata**
- Lens: ALCON Toric IOL — Clareon Toric Aspheric Hydrophobic Acrylic (Models CNW0T3–T9, CNA0T3–T9) and AcrySof Toric (Models SN6AT3–9, SA6AT3–9)
- Manufacturer: Alcon Laboratories, Inc.
- Category: Toric (astigmatism-correcting) monofocal IOL, aspheric surface enhancement
- PMA: Clareon-material version is covered under **P190018** (base approval, decided 2020-01-07). Per the brochure itself: *"Bench studies have demonstrated equivalent optical performance between AcrySof Toric IOLs and Clareon Toric IOLs. Therefore, the data presented in this section are applicable to both."* Note the AcrySof-material Toric line is a formally separate, older PMA family (P930014) — not independently re-verified this session, flagging the distinction rather than treating both as one approval.
- Source: FDA Patient Information Brochure "ALCON Toric Intraocular Lenses", `accessdata.fda.gov/cdrh_docs/pdf19/P190018D.pdf`, document TOR11343PE

**2. Indications**
Capsular bag implantation to replace the natural lens after cataract removal in adults who also have corneal astigmatism. Different models available for varying levels of astigmatism. Designed to optically correct corneal astigmatism and restore distance vision; also includes an aspheric surface enhancement for low-light distance vision (same benefit/caveat as the non-toric Aspheric Monofocal — requires glasses to realize).

**3. How it works**
A monofocal (single focal point) lens with a toric surface that corrects corneal astigmatism at the time of surgery. Does not provide near vision or extended depth of focus — patients still need reading glasses. Corrects astigmatism only when positioned correctly in the eye; if the lens rotates from its intended axis, correction is reduced.

**4. Expected visual outcomes**
From the FDA clinical study (toric arm, n=211, uncorrected distance vision at 6 months post-op):
- 20/40 or better without glasses: 93.8%
- 20/20 or better without glasses: 38.4%

(vs. non-toric/monofocal comparator: 77.1% and 19.0% respectively — see the Clareon Monofocal document.) Separately, a visual-distortion questionnaire (small subsample, n=14) found the rate of patients reporting trouble with things appearing distorted dropped from 78.6% before surgery to 14.3% at 6 months after — directionally useful but too small a sample to treat as a precise population statistic.

**5. Known side effects / visual disturbances** *(flagged for Martin's review — not auto-approved)*
From the clinical study safety data (N=244): retinal detachment/repair 0.4%, surgical reintervention 1.6% (two of these specifically due to toric-lens rotation), IOL repositioning due to rotation 0.4%, IOL replacement due to rotation 0.4%, laser treatment 0.8%, paracentesis 0.4%. The brochure states explicitly: **"Toric IOLs require surgical repositioning more often than non-toric IOLs."** If the lens isn't positioned correctly or rotates after surgery, patients may experience visual distortions (tilted lines, curved-looking floors) until repositioned. General cataract-surgery risks (infection, bleeding, inflammation, increased eye pressure) also apply.

**6. Contraindications / poor-candidate factors**
Safety and effectiveness not established in patients with pre-existing eye conditions or surgical complications such as glaucoma or diabetic retinopathy. Not implantable if surgical complications (e.g., capsule/zonule tissue damage) create a meaningful risk of post-op rotation. Outcome depends on the eye's health before surgery.

**7. Source citation**
Clareon: PMA P190018 (base). AcrySof (separate, older material line): PMA P930014 (not independently re-verified this session — confirm before publishing if this distinction matters for the specific model in use). Patient Information Brochure: `https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/cdrh_docs/pdf19/P190018D.pdf` (document TOR11343PE — Table 2 toric arm and Table 3 safety events cited above).
