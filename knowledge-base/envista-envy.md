# enVista Envy — Patient Labeling

**1. Metadata**
- Lens: enVista Envy™ hydrophobic acrylic IOL (non-toric, Models EN/EPN) and enVista Envy™ Toric hydrophobic acrylic IOL (Models ETN/ETPN)
- Manufacturer: Bausch & Lomb Incorporated
- Category: Trifocal (multifocal), presbyopia-correcting IOL, built on the enVista platform; toric model additionally corrects corneal astigmatism
- PMA: P240005 (original approval, decided 2024-10-10)
- **Market status note:** This lens was voluntarily recalled by Bausch & Lomb around April 2025 due to reports of toxic anterior segment syndrome (TASS) complications. Per Martin's confirmation, it is back on the market as of this writing — flagging this history explicitly since it's directly relevant to informed patient discussion, not something to omit.
- Source: FDA Summary of Safety and Effectiveness Data (SSED), `accessdata.fda.gov/cdrh_docs/pdf24/P240005B.pdf`

**2. Indications**
Non-toric (EN/EPN): primary implantation in the capsular bag for visual correction of aphakia in adults with ≤1.0D preoperative corneal astigmatism, after cataract removal — mitigates presbyopia by improving intermediate and near visual acuity while maintaining distance visual acuity comparable to an aspheric monofocal IOL. Toric (ETN/ETPN): same population plus correction of corneal astigmatism. Capsular bag placement only.

**3. How it works**
A one-piece, hydrophobic acrylic trifocal IOL. The anterior surface has an aspheric apodized diffractive optic; the posterior surface is aspheric refractive (or aspheric refractive toric for the toric model). Near add power +3.1D, intermediate add power +1.6D. A continuous 360° square posterior edge is designed to help prevent posterior capsular opacification. Unique fenestration holes in the haptics are designed to assist intraoperative positioning. UV-absorbing material.

**4. Expected visual outcomes**
From the FDA pivotal trial (enVista Trifocal IOL vs. enVista Monofocal IOL control, binocular, photopic conditions, Visit 4 at 4-6 months post-op):
- Best-corrected distance vision (BCDVA): 20/20 or better in 85.3% of trifocal subjects; 20/40 or better in 100% of subjects in both groups
- Uncorrected near vision (UNVA, 40cm): 20/20 or better in 38.4% of trifocal subjects vs. 0% of monofocal subjects; 20/40 or better in 99.3% vs. 31.5%
- Distance-corrected near vision (DCNVA, 40cm): 20/20 or better in 44.0% of trifocal subjects vs. 0% of monofocal subjects; 20/40 or better in 98.9% vs. 24.6%
- Distance-corrected intermediate vision (DCIVA, 66cm): 20/20 or better in 61.5% of trifocal subjects vs. 2.8% of monofocal subjects; 20/40 or better in 98.6% vs. a low percentage of monofocal subjects (exact figure not legible in the source PDF's text layer — flagging rather than guessing)
- Uncorrected intermediate vision (UIVA, 66cm): 20/20 or better in 51.8% of trifocal subjects; 20/40 or better in 98.6% vs. 84.6% of monofocal subjects

No spectacle-independence questionnaire data (of the kind reported for the Alcon/J&J trials in this knowledge base) was found in this SSED — not included here since it wasn't part of this trial's reported endpoints. Population-level trial data — not a guarantee of any individual patient's outcome.

**5. Known side effects / visual disturbances** *(flagged for Martin's review — not auto-approved)*
From the same trial's Quality of Vision questionnaire at 4-6 months:
- Halos reported "quite often or very often": 36.9% (116/314) of trifocal subjects (the SSED's PDF text layer was partially corrupted for the exact monofocal comparator figure in this specific stat — not fabricating a number here)
- About 10% more subjects (31/311) experienced glare and starbursts at least occasionally in the trifocal group vs. monofocal
- Moderate-to-severe difficulty with focusing and depth perception: reported by a higher proportion of trifocal subjects than monofocal subjects (5.5%, 15/310, for one specific comparator cited in the source) — the source text was partially corrupted for the full comparison; treat this as directionally accurate (trifocal higher than monofocal) rather than an exact percentage
- Contrast sensitivity was reduced in the trifocal group vs. monofocal for most tested conditions
- Ocular treatment-emergent adverse event rate: 49.4% of trifocal subjects vs. a comparable rate in the monofocal group (most common: punctate keratitis, increased intraocular pressure, vitreous detachment) — similar between groups, not multifocal-specific
- See the market-status note in §1 regarding the 2025 TASS-related recall

**6. Contraindications / poor-candidate factors**
FDA's SSED states there are no known contraindications specific to this device. General multifocal/trifocal-category cautions apply by extension, consistent with the other trifocal lenses in this knowledge base (irregular astigmatism, pre-existing retinal disease that could limit the benefit of a multifocal design).
