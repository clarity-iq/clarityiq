# ClarityIQ — General IOL Science Reference

Working document. Cross-lens optical and materials science — concepts that apply
across multiple IOL categories, not tied to any single lens. Per-lens facts
(FDA data, indications, specific outcomes) live in their own committed
documents per the per-lens template, not here.

**Source of this content:** carried forward from the original prototype
ElevenLabs system prompt (pre-commercial version), where it lived inline.
Relocated here so the conversation agent references a single committed
source instead of duplicating facts inside the prompt itself.

**Use:** the agent consults this when a patient asks a technical question
that isn't specific to one lens. Personal clinical application is always
redirected to the surgeon — this reference supports general education only.

## Lens materials

- Most modern IOLs are made of acrylic — either hydrophobic or hydrophilic acrylic.
- Hydrophobic acrylic has very low water content, excellent optical clarity,
  low PCO rates, and minimal glistening. Used in most premium lenses.
- Hydrophilic acrylic has higher water content and good biocompatibility,
  but slightly higher PCO rates in some studies.
- Silicone lenses are older technology, less commonly used now; not
  compatible with silicone oil if vitreoretinal surgery is ever needed.
- PMMA (polymethyl methacrylate) is rigid, requires a larger incision,
  rarely used in modern cataract surgery.

## Abbe number

- The Abbe number measures how much a material disperses light into its
  component colors (chromatic aberration).
- Higher Abbe number = less chromatic aberration = sharper, crisper color
  contrast.
- Standard acrylic IOLs typically have an Abbe number around 37–40.
- This is lower than the natural crystalline lens (Abbe ~58) and lower than
  glass.
- Chromatic aberration from a lower Abbe number can contribute to color
  fringing or slightly reduced contrast, particularly noticeable in
  high-contrast situations.
- Some patients perceive this more than others — it is one factor surgeons
  consider alongside other optical properties.

## Posterior capsule opacification (PCO)

- PCO is the most common complication after cataract surgery — sometimes
  called a "secondary cataract."
- It occurs when residual lens epithelial cells migrate onto the posterior
  capsule behind the IOL, causing gradual clouding.
- Hydrophobic acrylic lenses generally have lower PCO rates than hydrophilic
  acrylic in long-term studies.
- PCO is easily treated with a quick in-office laser procedure (YAG
  capsulotomy) — painless, takes minutes, fully restores clarity.
- It is not a sign that the surgery failed.

## Glistening

- Glistening refers to tiny fluid-filled microvacuoles that can form within
  some acrylic IOL materials over time.
- Most commonly reported with certain hydrophobic acrylic formulations.
- In the vast majority of patients, glistening does not affect vision and is
  only visible on examination.
- Subsurface nanoglistening is a related phenomenon — also typically
  subclinical.
- Manufacturers have reformulated materials over time to reduce glistening
  rates.

## Spherical aberration and wavefront optics

- The natural cornea introduces positive spherical aberration — it bends
  peripheral light rays slightly more than central ones.
- Standard spherical IOLs do not correct for this, which can reduce contrast
  sensitivity, especially in low light.
- Aspheric IOLs are designed with a modified surface that compensates for
  corneal spherical aberration, improving contrast sensitivity and night
  vision quality.
- The benefit of aspheric correction depends on the individual cornea —
  the surgeon measures this and factors it into lens selection.

## Extended depth of focus (EDOF) vs. trifocal vs. standard — mechanism-level comparison

- Standard monofocal: one focal point, no added glasses independence beyond
  that point.
- Toric monofocal: one focal point with astigmatism correction — sharper
  single-distance vision.
- EDOF: extends the range of focus compared to monofocal, typically strong
  for distance and intermediate, less robust at near. Fewer halos than
  traditional multifocal, but less near independence.
- Trifocal: three distinct focal points — distance, intermediate, near.
  Highest glasses independence among these categories, higher halo
  potential than EDOF.
- EDOF is sometimes positioned as a "middle ground" between monofocal and
  trifocal — specific lens availability depends on the practice.

## Blue light filtering

- Some IOLs include a blue light filter in the lens material (yellow-tinted
  lenses).
- Rationale: the natural crystalline lens filters blue light, and
  blue-light-filtering IOLs attempt to replicate this.
- Evidence on clinical benefit for retinal protection is mixed — some
  studies suggest benefit, others show no significant difference.
- Some patients prefer clear (non-filtering) lenses for color perception
  and low-light performance.
- Both are safe and widely used — surgeon preference is based on retinal
  health and lifestyle.

## Haptic design

- Haptics are the arms that extend from the optic (the clear central lens)
  and anchor the IOL in the capsular bag.
- One-piece IOLs have haptics made from the same acrylic material as the
  optic — generally preferred for in-the-bag placement.
- Three-piece IOLs have PMMA or prolene haptics — more versatile for sulcus
  fixation or special cases.
- Haptic design affects rotational stability — critical for toric lenses,
  where precise alignment determines astigmatism correction accuracy.
- Most modern premium IOLs use one-piece designs optimized for rotational
  stability.
