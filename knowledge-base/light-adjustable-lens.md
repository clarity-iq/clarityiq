# Light Adjustable Lens (LAL) — Patient Labeling

**1. Metadata**
- Lens: Light Adjustable Lens (LAL) with Light Delivery Device (LDD) — a two-part system, not the lens alone
- Manufacturer: RxSight, Inc.
- Category: Post-operatively adjustable IOL — a fundamentally different category from every other lens in this knowledge base. The lens itself doesn't determine final refractive power at the time of surgery; power is fine-tuned afterward using targeted UV light exposures, then permanently locked in.
- PMA: P160055 (original approval, decided 2017-11-22)
- Source: FDA Summary of Safety and Effectiveness Data (SSED), `accessdata.fda.gov/cdrh_docs/pdf16/P160055B.pdf`

**2. Indications**
Indicated for reduction of residual astigmatism to improve uncorrected visual acuity after cataract removal and IOL implantation, in adult patients with pre-existing corneal astigmatism ≥0.75D and without pre-existing macular disease. Also reduces the likelihood of clinically significant residual spherical refractive error. Requires a multi-visit postoperative process: after implantation, the patient returns for one or more "adjustment" visits (where the LDD delivers UV light to reshape the lens based on the eye's actual healed refraction) followed by a "lock-in" treatment that permanently stabilizes the final power. This is a materially different treatment course from every other lens in this knowledge base, which are implanted once with a fixed, pre-determined power.

**3. How it works**
A posterior chamber, UV-absorbing, three-piece, foldable photoreactive silicone IOL. The silicone material contains a photoreactive additive that selectively polymerizes in targeted areas when exposed to a spatially-profiled near-UV light from the separate Light Delivery Device, physically changing the lens's curvature and thereby its spherical or spherocylindrical power — after the lens is already implanted and the eye has healed enough to measure the true residual refractive error. A final "lock-in" UV exposure stabilizes the adjusted power permanently. The LDD can treat postoperative manifest cylinder from -0.75D to -2.00D and manifest sphere from -2.00D to +2.00D.

**4. Expected visual outcomes**
From the FDA pivotal trial (LAL n=403 vs. monofocal control n=197, at 6 months post-op, after adjustment and lock-in for the LAL group):
- Uncorrected distance vision (UCVA) 20/20 or better: **70.1% of LAL eyes vs. 36.3% of control eyes (P<.0001)** — the trial's key secondary effectiveness endpoint
- Manifest cylinder (astigmatism) reduction at 6 months: 73.4% for eyes with 0.75–1.25D of pre-treatment cylinder (vs. 18.6% for control), and 77.5% for eyes with >1.25D of pre-treatment cylinder (vs. 23.1% for control) — both differences highly statistically significant (P<.0001)
- Best-corrected distance vision (BSCVA) 20/40 or better: 100% of eyes in both groups at 6 and 12 months, exceeding the ISO historical control benchmark of 92.5%
- One effectiveness measure — percent reduction in spherical refractive error (MRSE) for eyes with minimal pre-treatment astigmatism — did not reach statistical superiority over the control group in this trial

Population-level trial data — not a guarantee of any individual patient's outcome. Because outcomes depend on a multi-visit adjustment process (not just the implant itself), how well this works also depends on completing that follow-up course as prescribed.

**5. Known side effects / visual disturbances** *(flagged for Martin's review — not auto-approved)*
This device has adverse-event categories not seen with any other lens in this knowledge base, tied specifically to its UV-light-based adjustment mechanism:
- **Secondary surgical intervention rate was significantly higher than the ISO historical control rate (P<.05):** 1.7% (7/403) of LAL eyes required a secondary surgical intervention (3 explants, one corneal transplant procedure [DSEK], two iris-adhesion treatments, one retinal laser procedure) vs. 0.5% (1/197) of control eyes
- **Phototoxic retinal damage:** one case (0.2%), caused by a faulty filter in the UV light source — RxSight took corrective action to prevent defective filters from reaching the market. This patient's best-corrected vision temporarily dropped to 20/150, recovering to 20/22 over about 5 months; the patient also developed a persistent tritan (blue-yellow) color vision defect that was still present 4 years later, and ultimately had the lens explanted and replaced with a standard monofocal IOL
- Persistent induced tritan color vision anomaly: 0.5% (2 cases, including the one above)
- Persistent induced erythropsia (a temporary or persistent reddish tinge to vision): 0.3% (1 case)
- Reactivation of ocular herpes simplex infection after UV treatment: 0.3% (1 case) — consistent with the contraindication for patients with a herpes simplex history (see §6)
- Persistent unanticipated significant increase in refractive error (≥1.0D): 1.3% (5 cases)
- Standard cataract-surgery adverse events (cystoid macular edema, hypopyon, endophthalmitis) occurred at low rates, generally within or below the ISO historical benchmark

**6. Contraindications / poor-candidate factors**
This device has a **formal FDA contraindications list**, driven by its UV-light treatment mechanism:
1. Patients taking systemic medications that increase UV sensitivity (e.g., tetracycline, doxycycline, psoralens, amiodarone, phenothiazines, chloroquine, hydrochlorothiazide, hypericin, ketoprofen, piroxicam, lomefloxacin, methoxsalen — a partial list; all medications should be reviewed for this effect before considering this lens)
2. Patients taking retina-toxic systemic medications (e.g., tamoxifen)
3. Patients with a history of ocular herpes simplex virus (UV exposure risks reactivation)
4. Patients with nystagmus (may be unable to maintain steady fixation during LDD treatment)
5. Patients unwilling or unable to comply with the postoperative adjustment/lock-in visit schedule and required UV-protective eyewear between visits
