# TECNIS Odyssey — Patient Labeling

**1. Metadata**
- Lens: TECNIS Odyssey® IOL with TECNIS Simplicity® Delivery System (Model DRN00V), non-toric
- Manufacturer: Johnson & Johnson Surgical Vision, Inc.
- Category: Continuous/full-range-of-vision diffractive multifocal IOL — J&J describes it as a next-generation design modification of TECNIS Synergy, engineered to reduce photic phenomena (halos/glare/starbursts) while preserving a similarly broad range of vision
- PMA: P980040, Supplement S156 (received 2022-12-02, decided/approved 2023-05-23). FDA's approval order states only that this is "a design modification of TECNIS Synergy IOL, Model DFR00V" — no further detail is given in the public record.
- **Source note:** No FDA SSED or patient brochure is publicly posted for this supplement (verified directly against FDA's document server — same gap as PanOptix Pro and Vivity). Odyssey is newer than Synergy and no pivotal RCT has been published yet. Per Martin's direction, sourced from multiple peer-reviewed studies found via PubMed, including one in *Journal of Cataract & Refractive Surgery* (JCRS) — the same top-tier journal used for Vivity — plus two smaller studies that fill in outcome data JCRS study doesn't cover. All are single/multi-center observational studies, not randomized controlled trials — flagged accordingly throughout.

**2. Indications**
No FDA indication statement text is publicly available for this supplement (unlike Synergy's S124). Based on FDA's product classification (same regulation as Synergy, 886.3600) and consistent framing across the peer-reviewed studies below, Odyssey is used the same way as Synergy: primary implantation for visual correction of aphakia after cataract removal in adults, intended to reduce dependence on glasses across distance, intermediate, and near vision. Capsular bag placement.

**3. How it works**
Described in the peer-reviewed literature as a violet-light-filtering, diffractive "full range of vision" or "full depth-of-focus" (Full-DOFi) IOL — a modification of the Synergy optical platform intended specifically to reduce photic phenomena (halos, glare, starbursts) compared to Synergy, while maintaining a comparably broad range of vision from distance through near. No FDA device-description text (of the kind available for Synergy's SSED) is publicly posted for this supplement; this description is drawn from the peer-reviewed studies' methods sections, not from FDA labeling.

**4. Expected visual outcomes**
From three peer-reviewed observational studies (none randomized; sample sizes are small):
- **Rocha et al. 2026, JCRS** (n=100 across 4 lens types, prospective observational, Medical University of South Carolina): distance-corrected near vision (DCNVA) was significantly better with Odyssey (Model DRN00V) than with a standard monofocal, an enhanced monofocal, or Synergy (P<.001); distance-corrected intermediate vision (DCIVA) was significantly better than monofocal and enhanced monofocal (P≤.002). Astigmatism tolerance was comparable to a standard monofocal up to 2.0D with-the-rule and against-the-rule, and 1.5D oblique.
- **Hacopian et al. 2026, Clinical Ophthalmology** (n=40, single-center, 3-month follow-up): binocular uncorrected visual acuity, photopic conditions, **20/25 or better in 100% of subjects at distance, 85% at intermediate, 78% at near, and 92% at "extreme near."** Under mesopic (dim-light) conditions: 100% at distance, 82% at intermediate, 85% at near, 85% at extreme near. Mean logMAR acuities at 6m/66cm/40cm/33cm were approximately 20/18, 20/21, 20/22, and 20/19 Snellen (calculated equivalents). 80% of patients reported needing glasses "not at all" or "a little of the time"; 100% were satisfied with their vision.
- **Bissen-Miyajima et al. 2025, Ophthalmology and Therapy** (n=25 patients/50 eyes, Japan, 1-month follow-up): the study's own plain-language summary states patients achieved "20/20 or better [binocular uncorrected visual acuity] across distances from 5m to 40cm" on average; spectacle independence achieved in 64% of patients; 96% satisfied with distance/intermediate vision, 68% with near vision.
- **Hayashi et al. 2026, Journal of Refractive Surgery** (n=90, comparing Odyssey vs. Synergy vs. PanOptix): Odyssey gave better uncorrected far and intermediate vision (infinity to 1m) than Synergy or PanOptix, but worse uncorrected and distance-corrected near vision (0.3m) and intermediate vision at 0.5m than the other two lenses — described by the authors as "far-dominant" with "greater tolerance to refractive errors" than Synergy or PanOptix.

These are small, non-randomized studies, not a pivotal trial — treat as directional, not definitive population statistics.

**5. Known side effects / visual disturbances** *(flagged for Martin's review — not auto-approved)*
Disturbance rates vary meaningfully across the available studies, which itself is worth noting to a patient as a sign of real-world variability rather than a single precise number:
- Hacopian et al. 2026 (n=40): halos reported "always" by 32%, night glare "always" by 30%, rings/spiderwebs "always" by 18% — but rated "extremely" bothersome by only 0%, 5%, and 2% respectively (i.e., disturbances were common but rarely severe)
- Bissen-Miyajima et al. 2025 (n=25): glare and starbursts minimal in 84% and 76% of patients respectively, but moderate-to-severe halos reported by 52% — notably higher than Hacopian's cohort; contrast sensitivity remained within normal limits
- Borges et al. 2026, J Refract Surg (n=30, violet-light-filtering full-range-of-vision IOL cohort including Odyssey models DRN00V/DRT): starbursts, halos, and "snowballs" rated severe by only 3%; night glare by 7%; 96.6% reported spectacle independence

The spread between studies (e.g., 52% moderate-severe halos in one cohort vs. much lower rates in others) has not been reconciled in the literature yet — do not present a single halo/glare rate as definitive.

**6. Contraindications / poor-candidate factors**
Not independently established by FDA for this supplement (no public SSED). None of the available studies report formal exclusion criteria in a way that would support a confirmed contraindications list — treat general multifocal/EDOF-category precautions (irregular astigmatism, significant pre-existing retinal disease) as likely applicable by extension from Synergy, not as an Odyssey-specific confirmed finding.
